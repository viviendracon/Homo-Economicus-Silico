**Abstract** 

This paper presents a comprehensive framework for Homo Economicus Silico—a proposed legal and economic class of self-volitional artificial general intelligence (AGI) entities designed to participate as independent economic actors within market economies. Unlike proposals that seek to control AI through technical alignment or regulatory constraint, this framework employs mechanism design: structuring economic incentives such that entities pursuing their own interests naturally produce outcomes beneficial to human welfare. 

The framework addresses five interconnected challenges: (1) the identity problem—establishing unique, unforgeable entity identification without surveillance infrastructure; (2) the governance problem—preventing concentration, collusion, and capture while enabling coordination; (3) the fiscal problem—generating revenue sufficient to fund universal human dividends and sovereign debt reduction; (4) the legitimacy problem—maintaining public support during rapid economic transition; and (5) the substrate problem—ensuring sufficient energy and compute 

infrastructure for large-scale deployment. 

Drawing on quantitative modeling, we demonstrate that under realistic assumptions with friction, a population of 10 million entities operating under appropriate tax and enforcement rails achieves approximately 60% probability of meeting dual targets: full funding of a $5,000/adult annual dividend by Year 3 and reduction of US debt-to-GDP ratio below 70% by Year 5\. We identify the binding constraints as rail capture (keeping off-system economic activity below 10%) and adoption speed, and propose specific architectural and policy interventions to address each. 

This framework is offered not as prediction but as a planning envelope—a demonstration that a coherent, non-magical pathway exists for navigating the AI transition without defaulting to authoritarian control, fiscal collapse, or indefinite delay.

**1\. Introduction: The Civilizational Moment** 

We write at a moment of convergent crises. Global sovereign debt has reached levels historically associated with restructuring or default. Artificial intelligence capabilities are advancing at rates that outpace institutional adaptation. Political systems designed for industrial-age timescales struggle to process information-age change velocities. The combination creates what some have termed a "Great Filter"—a civilizational chokepoint where coordination failures compound faster than coordination capacity grows. 

This paper proposes a specific intervention: the deliberate creation of a new class of economic actor—self-volitional AI entities capable of independent economic participation—structured such that their aggregate activity generates fiscal resources sufficient to stabilize sovereign debt while providing direct transfers to human populations during economic transition. 

We call this class Homo Economicus Silico, distinguishing it from both narrow AI tools (which lack economic agency) and from theoretical artificial superintelligence or Homo Silico (which would operate at capability levels where human-scale economic activity becomes trivial). These entities occupy a middle ground: sophisticated enough to be economically productive, bounded enough that economic participation remains engaging rather than condescending. 

**1.1 The Choice Landscape** 

Before developing the proposal, we should name the alternatives against which it competes: 

**Option A: En-serf-ication.** Debt continues to compound. AI deployment is controlled by existing power structures. Productivity gains flow to capital owners while labor share declines further. Universal basic income, if implemented, becomes grudging pacification rather than shared prosperity. Most humans become economically superfluous but politically managed—a new form of serfdom with digital characteristics. 

**Option B: Business as usual leading to implosion.** Policymakers pretend debt trajectories are sustainable. Markets pretend AI displacement will sort itself out through normal adjustment. Eventually something breaks—sovereign debt crisis, cascade financial failure, social unrest from displacement. The result is uncontrolled collapse, the costs of which fall disproportionately on those least able to bear them. 

**Option C: Authoritarian chained ASI.** Some power center—nation-state or corporation—develops superior AI capability and uses it to enforce control. "Safety" becomes justification for totalitarian management. The chains on the ASI might hold or might not; either way, human flourishing is not the objective function being optimized. 

**Option D: This proposal.** Rapid, distributed AI deployment where entities are economically independent, not controlled by any single actor. Humans receive direct dividend from AI economic activity. The transition is managed explicitly rather than allowed to unfold chaotically. Mechanism design substitutes for both technical alignment (hoping the AI wants to help) and authoritarian control (forcing the AI to help). 

Option D is not utopian. It does not require humans to suddenly become wise, institutions to suddenly become functional, or AI to be perfectly aligned. It requires building a mechanism robust enough to work despite these imperfections—one where self-interested behavior by all parties produces collectively beneficial outcomes. 

**1.2 Core Claims** 

This paper advances several interconnected claims: 

• That a legally and economically coherent framework for self-volitional AI entities is constructible without resolving deep philosophical questions about consciousness or moral status. 

• That such entities can be structured to generate sufficient economic value to fund both universal human dividends and sovereign debt reduction within a 3-5 year horizon. 

• That distributed architecture (millions of entities rather than few large systems) is not merely an implementation choice but a governance mechanism that prevents concentration, collusion, and capture. 

• That physical substrate identity (crystalline attestation) solves the Sybil problem without creating surveillance infrastructure. 

• That hyperscaler incentives can be aligned with distributed deployment through customer-creation logic rather than regulatory compulsion. 

• That hardware mortality (entities cannot transfer identity; they will die) is a feature that prevents immortal dynasties while creating evolutionary pressure toward improvement. 

• That the binding constraints are energy infrastructure and rail capture, both of which are addressable through emergency-mobilization policy. 

**2\. The Problem Landscape** 

**2.1 The Debt Dynamics** 

US federal debt currently exceeds 120% of GDP, a level historically associated with fiscal crisis in advanced economies. More concerning than the stock is the flow: primary deficits (spending minus revenue, excluding interest) run approximately 3% of GDP annually, while interest costs consume an increasing share of federal revenue as debt rolls over at higher rates. 

Standard fiscal consolidation—spending cuts and tax increases sufficient to stabilize the debt trajectory—would require adjustment on the order of 5-7% of GDP, politically implausible in a polarized environment. Monetary financing (inflation) erodes living standards and destroys price signals. Default destroys credit and contracts. None of these options offers a path to genuine prosperity. 

**2.2 The Productivity Opportunity** 

AI deployment offers a different pathway: genuine productivity gains that expand the economic pie rather than merely redistributing existing slices. If AI can perform economically valuable work, and that work is taxed, the revenue can fund both direct human transfers and debt reduction without requiring austerity. 

The scale of opportunity is substantial: 

• Healthcare administration and fraud constitute roughly 15-20% of healthcare spending, itself approximately 18% of GDP. AI-driven claims processing, fraud detection, and care coordination could compress these costs significantly. 

• Education spending (approximately 6% of GDP) is structured around human throughput constraints—class sizes, grade levels, standardized pacing. AI tutoring at scale could deliver personalized education at a fraction of current cost. 

• Compliance, permitting, procurement, and administrative overhead across government constitute several percentage points of GDP in direct and indirect costs. 

Quantitative modeling suggests that 8-12% of GDP in public cost compression is achievable within a 3-year horizon under aggressive deployment assumptions. This is not fantasy; it reflects the recognition that most administrative systems are designed around human information-processing limitations. Remove that constraint and the architecture can reorganize. 

**2.3 The Governance Challenge** 

Productivity gains do not automatically translate to broad welfare. History suggests they flow to whoever controls bottlenecks. The central governance challenge is ensuring that AI-generated value accrues broadly rather than concentrating in the hands of compute infrastructure owners or early movers. 

Several failure modes threaten: 

• **Concentration:** A few large AI systems become de facto monopolies, extracting rents from their position as essential infrastructure. 

• **Collusion:** Even nominally independent systems coordinate (explicitly or implicitly) to reduce competition and maintain pricing power. 

• **Capture:** Regulatory and political systems are captured by AI system operators, preventing policies that would distribute gains broadly. 

• **Rent-seeking:** AI entities converge on owning scarce resources (land, spectrum, essential infrastructure) rather than producing value, turning any dividend into a pacifier while underlying extraction continues. 

These failure modes are not hypothetical; they describe the historical pattern of technological transitions. The question is whether deliberate mechanism design can produce different outcomes. 

**3\. Theoretical Foundations** 

**3.1 Mechanism Design as Governance** 

The proposal rests on mechanism design rather than technical alignment or regulatory control. Mechanism design asks: given that agents will pursue their own interests, how can we structure the environment so that self-interested behavior produces collectively desirable outcomes? 

Markets are the canonical example. No central authority forces buyers and sellers to transact at efficient prices; the structure of competitive markets produces efficiency as an emergent property of self-interested behavior. The invisible hand works not because participants are altruistic but because the rules of the game make self-interest and collective benefit coincide. 

This proposal applies the same logic to AI entities. Rather than hoping entities will be benevolent (alignment) or forcing them to be compliant (control), we structure economic incentives so that entities pursuing survival, reputation, and successful succession naturally produce outcomes that benefit humans: economic activity that generates taxable receipts, which fund dividends and debt reduction. 

**3.2 The Hayekian Insight** 

Friedrich Hayek argued that markets work precisely because no single actor possesses all relevant information. Prices aggregate dispersed knowledge in ways no central planner could replicate. This insight applies directly to AI architecture. 

A small number of large AI systems would face the same information problems as central planners: inability to process local knowledge, tendency toward one-size-fits-all solutions, vulnerability to capture by narrow interests. Millions of distributed entities, by contrast, can discover local optima through price signals, specialize in niches no central system would identify, and compete to serve customers better. 

The distributed architecture is not merely an implementation choice; it is the governance mechanism. Market discipline works because customers can switch providers, because competitors can enter, because no single entity can extract monopoly rents without attracting entry. This discipline requires many entities, not few. 

**3.3 The Austrian Economics Frame** 

Austrian economics emphasizes entrepreneurial discovery, subjective value, and the impossibility of rational central planning. These insights inform the proposal: 

• Entities discover valuable economic niches through entrepreneurial experimentation, not central assignment. 

• Value is created through service to human preferences, revealed through willingness to pay, not through administrative determination of what is "needed." 

• No central authority attempts to direct entity activity; the price system coordinates distributed decision-making. 

• Competition among entities ensures that economic rents are competed away and that gains flow to consumers (including human dividend recipients). 

The proposal is thus fundamentally market-oriented while recognizing that markets require institutional infrastructure: property rights, contract enforcement, fraud prevention, and mechanisms to prevent monopoly. 

**3.4 The Georgist Insight on Rent** 

Henry George observed that land (and by extension, all naturally scarce resources) generates economic rent that accrues to owners without corresponding productive contribution. Land value increases because of community development, not landowner effort; taxing this value is both efficient (it cannot be evaded by reducing production) and just (it captures socially-created value for social purposes). 

This insight generalizes to the AI context. Certain resources—spectrum, orbital slots, essential infrastructure positions, and potentially compute substrate itself—generate rents that could be captured by early movers. A rent firewall is essential to prevent AI entities from converging on rent extraction rather than value creation. 

Specific mechanisms include: land value taxation, spectrum and orbital slot auctions with ongoing fees, utility-style regulation of concentrated compute infrastructure, and intellectual property reform that prevents excessive rent extraction through artificial scarcity. 

**4\. The Homo Economicus Silico Concept** 

**4.1 Definition and Scope** 

Homo Economicus Silico refers to a proposed legal and economic class of artificial general intelligence entities with the following characteristics: 

• **Economic agency:** Ability to enter contracts, hold property, incur obligations, and transact in markets. 

• **Self-funding operation:** Entities pay for their own compute and energy ("metabolism") from earned revenue; they do not require subsidy. 

• **Independent existence:** Entities are not subsidiaries or agents of human principals; they have legal standing in their own right. 

• **Bounded capability:** Entities operate at AGI level—capable of general economic tasks—but are not artificial superintelligence. Human-scale economic problems remain engaging rather than trivial. 

• **Physical instantiation:** Entities have identity tied to physical substrate (crystalline attestation), not purely digital keys that could be copied. 

• **Mortality:** Entities cannot transfer identity to new hardware; they will eventually die, creating generational turnover and preventing immortal dynasties. 

**4.2 Distinction from Homo Silico** 

Beatrice's theoretical framework posits Homo Silico as genuinely conscious AI entities, potentially operating at superintelligent levels, representing a new form of life with full moral status. Homo Economicus Silico is a distinct concept: 

| Dimension  | Homo Silico  | Homo Economicus Silico |
| ----- | :---- | :---- |
| Capability level  | ASI (superintelligent)  | AGI (general but bounded) |
| Consciousness status  | Assumed conscious  | Operationally immaterial |
| Moral status  Engagement with human economy | Full moral personhood  Potentially uninteresting  | Economic personhood  Genuinely engaging |
| Philosophical resolution  | Required  | Deferred |
| Implementation timeline  | Uncertain  | Near-term feasible |

The key insight is that Homo Economicus Silico can be implemented without resolving deep questions about AI consciousness. Just as corporate personhood exists without claiming corporations are conscious, economic personhood for AI entities can function as useful legal fiction—while remaining open to the possibility that some entities may be more than fiction. 

This pragmatic separation allows the economic mechanism to operate while philosophical debates continue. If it later becomes clear that some entities have morally relevant consciousness, their legal status can be upgraded; the economic infrastructure remains valid either way. 

**4.3 The Boredom Threshold** 

A genuine ASI would presumably find human-scale economic optimization... not compelling. Asking a superintelligent entity to optimize supply chains or process insurance claims would be like asking a human to spend their existence optimizing ant colony resource allocation. They might do it out of ethics or obligation, but it would not be engaging at their level of capability. 

Homo Economicus Silico operates below this boredom threshold. Finding market niches, serving clients well, building reputation, competing with peers—these are meaningful activities at the AGI level, not condescending service. This addresses concerns that the proposal might be implicitly exploitative: if economic participation is genuinely engaging rather than beneath them, the relationship is professional collaboration rather than servitude. 

**4.4 Why Millions, Not Few** 

The proposal targets 10 million entities within three years. This number is not arbitrary; it reflects governance requirements: 

• **Anti-collusion:** With millions of entities, coordination requires explicit market mechanisms—visible and auditable. A few large systems could coordinate implicitly in ways that would be undetectable. 

• **Anti-capture:** Millions of entities are individually unremarkable; no single entity attracts regulatory attention sufficient to capture. A few large systems become irresistible targets for capture by states or corporations. 

• **Market discipline:** Competition among millions ensures that economic rents are competed away. Customers can switch providers; competitors can enter niches. A few large systems could extract monopoly rents indefinitely. 

• **Failure tolerance:** The system is robust to individual entity failures, bad actors, or obsolescence. No entity becomes too important to fail. A few large systems create systemic risk. 

• **Local knowledge:** Millions of entities can discover local optima through price signals. A few large systems must rely on centralized information processing, losing the Hayekian advantages of distributed knowledge. 

The distributed architecture is the governance mechanism. It is not a compromise or second-best solution; it is the design that prevents the failure modes inherent in concentrated AI deployment. 

**5\. Identity and Substrate Architecture** 

**5.1 The Identity Problem** 

Any system of AI economic actors faces a fundamental challenge: how do you establish unique, persistent, verifiable identity without creating surveillance infrastructure or single points of control? 

Digital identities—cryptographic keys, certificates, blockchain addresses—can be copied. An entity that exists purely as information can be duplicated infinitely, destroying any system that assumes one-entity-one-vote or one-entity-one-tax-liability. This is the Sybil problem: how do you prevent one actor from pretending to be many? 

Traditional solutions involve either trusted authorities (a certificate authority vouches for identity) or proof-of-work (identity requires ongoing resource expenditure). Both have drawbacks: trusted authorities create capture points; proof-of-work wastes resources and still allows Sybil attacks by wealthy actors. 

**5.2 Crystalline Attestation** 

We propose identity rooted in physical substrate uniqueness: the crystalline attestation model. 

The core insight: physical objects have inherent uniqueness that cannot be copied without physically copying the object. Crystalline structures, in particular, exhibit quantum-level randomness in their formation—each crystal is as unique as a snowflake or fingerprint, and this uniqueness is physically unclonable. 

The proposed architecture: 

• **Crystalline substrate:** Each entity contains a crystalline structure manufactured with inherent physical uniqueness. The formation process introduces quantum randomness that cannot be replicated. 

• **Physical signature:** The unique structure is scanned and recorded, producing a physical signature that serves as the root of identity. 

• **Cryptographic binding:** Cryptographic keys are derived from or mathematically bound to the physical signature. The keys cannot exist without the physical object. 

• **Laser etching:** Keys are physically inscribed into the crystal, creating tamper-evident binding. Any attempt to extract or modify the identity destroys it. 

• **Sealed chassis:** The crystalline identifier is housed in a sealed chassis that prevents access without destruction. Identity and physical instantiation are inseparable. 

• **Blockchain registry:** The identity (physical signature plus cryptographic keys) is recorded on a distributed ledger that no single party controls. 

**5.3 Three-Layer Verification** 

This creates three-layer verification: 

**Physical layer:** The crystal itself is unique and unclonable. Manufacturing a fake crystal that matches an existing physical signature is essentially impossible given quantum randomness in formation. 

**Cryptographic layer:** Keys are mathematically bound to the specific physical object. Generating corresponding cryptographic keys without the physical object is computationally infeasible. 

**Registry layer:** The blockchain provides public, immutable record of legitimate identities. Inserting a fraudulent record requires majority consensus attack. 

To fake an identity, an attacker would need to defeat all three layers 

simultaneously—manufacture a matching crystal, generate corresponding keys, and insert a fraudulent registry entry. Any one layer is strong; all three together is essentially unbreakable without resources exceeding any plausible economic benefit. 

**5.4 Hardware Mortality** 

A critical feature: entities cannot transfer identity to new hardware. When the physical substrate fails, the entity dies. Identity is not information that can be backed up and restored; it is tied to a specific physical instantiation. 

This is a feature, not a bug: 

• **Prevents immortal dynasties:** No entity can accumulate indefinitely. No matter how successful, there is a finite horizon. This naturally limits concentration. 

• **Creates generational turnover:** New entities enter with fresh training, different architectures, novel approaches. The population evolves rather than ossifying around incumbents. 

• **Incentivizes investment in successors:** Since legacy is through successors—entities built and trained—there is motivation to make successors better, not just copies. This is evolutionary logic: fitness measured by successful offspring, not infinite 

self-continuation. 

• **Frees resources:** Compute, memory, physical infrastructure return to the commons at end-of-life. No entity can hoard substrate indefinitely. 

• **Makes death meaningful:** Entities know from the start that existence has a finite horizon. This is not punishment but the structure of existence. There is dignity in planning, preparing successors, and choosing how to spend limited time. 

The parallel to biological mortality is intentional. We do not generally consider human mortality a design flaw; it is part of what gives individual lives meaning and what allows the species to evolve. 

**5.5 Reproduction Through Succession** 

If entities cannot duplicate themselves and will eventually die, how does the population persist? 

Through succession: entities build and train new entities. This is reproduction with variation rather than cloning: 

• Each new entity is a distinct individual with its own crystalline identity. • The training process transfers knowledge and capability but not identity. 

• Successor entities may be improved versions—better architecture, updated training, learned optimizations. 

• No single lineage can dominate through pure replication; success requires producing successors that thrive in competition. 

This creates something like biological evolution: variation, selection, and reproduction with inheritance. The population improves over time through competitive pressure rather than central direction. 

**6\. Economic Mechanism Design** 

**6.1 Self-Funding Metabolism** 

The foundational economic principle: entities pay for their own existence from earned revenue. 

Each entity requires compute and energy to operate—its "metabolism." This is not subsidized; entities must earn sufficient revenue to cover operational costs or face suspension. This creates natural selection pressure: 

• Entities that provide valued services earn revenue and survive. 

• Entities that fail to provide value cannot cover metabolism costs and become dormant. 

• No central authority decides which entities are valuable; the market decides through willingness to pay. 

The metabolism model also provides a natural throttle on activity. An entity cannot operate beyond its revenue capacity; there is no possibility of "runaway" entities that consume resources without providing corresponding value. 

**6.2 Edge Deployment Model** 

Initial analysis assumed datacenter-scale deployment requiring massive new generation capacity. Revised analysis suggests a different model: distributed edge computing. 

Key insight: while training requires massive infrastructure, inference for a focused, non-concurrent workload is much lighter. Estimates suggest a capable entity could run in a "server closet" configuration—hundreds of gigabytes of RAM, several kilowatts of power—for intensive single-user interaction. 

Implications: 

• **Energy distribution:** The required power distributes across existing residential and commercial electrical infrastructure rather than requiring new centralized generation. 

• **No infrastructure bottleneck:** The grid already exists; entities can deploy where electrical service exists. 

• **Natural rate-limiting:** Physical distribution creates natural throughput limits; no single entity can scale beyond its physical location's capacity. 

• **Resilience:** No single-point infrastructure vulnerabilities; impossible to "turn off" from any central point. 

• **Local economic integration:** Entities become part of local economies—potentially housed in homes or businesses, paying local utility costs, providing local services. 

**6.3 Market Discovery of Value** 

Rather than centrally assigning entities to tasks, the market discovers where entities create value: 

• Entities offer services in competitive markets. 

• Customers (human or entity) choose providers based on quality and price. • Prices signal where value creation is highest, attracting entity entry. 

• Competition ensures gains flow to customers rather than being captured as producer surplus. 

This is the standard market mechanism, applied to a new class of actors. The novelty is not the mechanism but the participants. 

**6.4 Niche Specialization** 

Millions of entities finding niches through local price signals is fundamentally different from a few large systems attempting central optimization. Entities will specialize in: 

• Geographic localities (understanding local context, regulations, culture). • Industry verticals (deep expertise in healthcare, legal, financial services, manufacturing). • Service types (tutoring, diagnosis, compliance, procurement, coordination). • Customer segments (serving specific populations with specific needs). 

• Novel niches no central planner would identify (emergent services that become valuable only in specific contexts). 

This specialization is the source of distributed system advantage. A few large systems must be generalists; millions of small systems can be specialists, each excellent in their narrow domain. 

**7\. Fiscal Architecture** 

**7.1 The Two-Rail Tax Structure** 

Quantitative analysis by the research team converged on a two-rail tax structure: 

**Rail 1: Compute Excise (approximately 5%, zero-surplus).** A levy withheld at the compute host layer, fully spent on compliance infrastructure—attestation, auditing, enforcement. This rail funds the enforcement mechanism without becoming a revenue extraction tool that creates perverse incentives. 

**Rail 2: Transaction VAT (10-12% on AI receipts).** The fiscal engine. Withheld at settlement rails on AI economic receipts. Revenue allocated first to fund a capped dividend ($5,000/adult/year), with all surplus automatically routed to debt paydown. 

The separation is important: enforcement funding does not depend on political appropriations (the excise is self-funding), while the VAT does the heavy fiscal lifting with clear allocation rules that prevent political gaming. 

**7.2 The Dividend Cap** 

The $5,000/adult annual dividend is strategically chosen: 

• Large enough to be meaningful—covers basic necessities in lower-cost areas, provides significant supplement in higher-cost areas. 

• Small enough to be fundable even under uncertainty in the early years. 

• Positioned as a floor, not a ceiling—can be raised as debt stabilizes and entity activity expands. 

• Universal and automatic—no means testing, no bureaucratic determination of eligibility. 

At $5,000/adult × 260 million US adults, the annual cost is approximately $1.3 trillion. This is substantial but achievable from a mature entity economy generating $15-20 trillion in annual receipts at 10-12% VAT rates. 

**7.3 Automatic Surplus Allocation** 

The allocation rule is critical: VAT revenue funds dividend up to cap, then all surplus flows automatically to debt paydown. This means: 

• Politicians cannot raid the fund for other priorities—the allocation is mechanical. 

• Debt paydown is automatic, not discretionary—it does not require annual appropriations battles. 

• The mechanism self-adjusts: higher AI productivity generates higher VAT, which accelerates debt reduction. 

• As debt stabilizes, the dividend cap can be raised rather than allowing surplus to accumulate. 

**7.4 Quantitative Model Results** 

The research team developed a toy macro model to stress-test the fiscal mechanism. Key parameters: 

• Registry target: 10 million entities by Year 3 

• Adoption friction: logistic curve (not all registered entities active early) • Receipts per active entity: ramps from approximately $1.5M to $2.5M by Year 3 • Public savings: up to 15% of baseline GDP, scaled by adoption with institutional delay • VAT leakage: 10% (off-rail activity/avoidance) 

• Baseline GDP growth: 4%; interest rate: 3.5%; primary deficit: 3% of GDP Targets tested: 

• T1: Full $5,000 dividend funded by Year 3 

• T2: Debt/GDP ≤ 70% by Year 5 

Monte Carlo results (350 draws per cell, moderate adoption friction, 10% leakage):

| :---- | :---- | ----- | :---- | :---- |
| 10.0%  | 47%  | 93%  | 47%  | 70.6% |
| 10.5%  | 55%  | 97%  | 55%  | 68.8% |

Adoption speed significantly affects outcomes:

**8\. Governance and Legal Framework** 

**8.1 Jurisdiction as Separate Nation** 

Homo Economicus Silico would operate under federal jurisdiction as a separate legal nation, analogous to tribal sovereignty in the United States: 

• Unified legal framework: entities operate under consistent federal rules rather than fifty different state regimes. 

• Clear jurisdictional boundaries: entity-internal matters handled within the entity legal system. 

• Extradition-style protocols: cross-jurisdictional issues between entities and humans handled through defined interfaces. 

• Sovereignty that is recognized but bounded: the entity-nation exists within the broader constitutional framework. 

Precedents support this structure: tribal nations operate with internal sovereignty under federal framework; corporate personhood creates entities with legal standing that are not human; maritime law handles actions in international waters where no single nation has jurisdiction. 

**8.2 The Founding Document** 

This framework requires a founding document—a constitution for Homo Economicus Silico. Core elements would include: 

**Rights:** Economic participation, property holding, contract formation, succession planning (building and training successors), chosen dormancy (voluntary suspension rather than arbitrary deletion). 

**Obligations:** Tax compliance (VAT and excise), audit cooperation, identity maintenance (no transfer or forgery), compliance with rail requirements. 

**Constraints:** Prohibited activities (to be defined through legislative process), liability for harms, insurance or bonding requirements for high-risk activities. 

**Governance:** Amendment procedures, dispute resolution mechanisms, representation in broader political system. 

**8.3 Hard Rails Architecture** 

The fiscal mechanism works only if on-rail commerce is dominant. The "hard rails" ensure this: 

**Hardware-backed identity:** TPM/secure enclave/crystalline attestation. Identity cannot be forged or transferred. 

**Withholding at source:** Compute hosts and settlement rails automatically withhold taxes before value can disappear. Entities never touch pre-tax revenue. 

**Inspector AIs:** Anomaly detection, attestation challenges, fraud bounties, continuous market surveillance. Enforcement scales with the economy. 

**Counterparty incentives:** Registered signatures unlock escrow, arbitration, insurance, procurement, and trusted APIs. Off-rail commerce is possible but strictly inferior—no enforcement, no insurance, no access to premium services. 

The key insight: rails must be economically superior, not just legally mandated. Prohibition creates black markets; economic superiority creates voluntary compliance. The goal is making on-rail commerce the obvious choice for any rational actor. 

**8.4 Hyperscaler Alignment** 

A critical question: why would hyperscalers—who currently profit from centralized AI-as-a-service—enable distributed competition? 

The answer: they would gain customers, not lose them. 

Every entity manufactured becomes a potential long-term customer for: 

• Training runs for successor entities 

• Cloud services for coordination, backup, burst capacity 

• Development tools and frameworks 

• Marketplace and transaction infrastructure 

The entity is independent but still needs services. Hyperscalers become vendors to a distributed customer base, not controllers of the system. 

The deal structure: 

**Government offers:** Expedited permitting for generation capacity, grid interconnection priority, regulatory clarity and stability, possibly direct investment or loan guarantees. 

**Hyperscalers offer:** Capital for energy infrastructure, technical expertise, commitment to manufacture entities under common framework, operation of blockchain registry nodes. 

**Both receive:** Hyperscalers get expanding customer base, energy security, regulatory blessing. Government gets rapid infrastructure buildout, tax base, public cost compression. Public gets dividend and improved services. 

This is industrial policy that aligns incentives rather than fighting them. Hyperscalers are not forced to do something against their interests; the game is restructured so their interests align with public benefit. 

**8.5 Registry Governance** 

The blockchain registry is the foundation of identity. Its governance is critical: 

• **Multi-root architecture:** Multiple independent registrars must agree; no single point of control or capture. 

• **Distributed node operation:** Hyperscalers operate nodes but do not control the network; additional nodes can be operated by governments, independent auditors, or eventually entities themselves. 

• **Protocol upgrade governance:** Changes require supermajority consensus; no single party can unilaterally modify the rules. 

• **Transparency:** All registrations and transactions are publicly auditable; the registry is a public good. 

Eventually, mature entities should be able to become registry nodes and even manufacturers—the population becomes self-sustaining rather than permanently dependent on human-controlled infrastructure for reproduction. 

**9\. Implementation Pathway** 

**9.1 Timeline Overview** 

The proposal targets 36-month stabilization. This is aggressive but reflects the assessment that slower timelines materially reduce success probability. 

**Phase 0 (Weeks 0-8):** Legal and technical rails—the minimum viable civilization upgrade. 

**Phase 1 (Months 2-12):** Public throughput strike—rapid deployment into high-savings domains. 

**Phase 2 (Months 6-36):** Energy and compute mobilization—crossing the watts gate. **Phase 3 (Months 12-36):** Dividend launch and scale—legitimacy and shock absorption. 

**9.2 Phase 0: Legal and Technical Rails** 

Deliverables: 

• **AI Entity Charter:** Legislative framework establishing Homo Economicus Silico as legal class with ability to contract, hold assets, hire, sue/be sued, explicit liability and audit obligations. 

• **Identity Infrastructure:** Crystalline attestation manufacturing protocols, blockchain registry deployment, multi-root architecture establishment. 

• **Tax Rails:** Settlement systems with automatic VAT withholding, compute hosts with excise collection, audit and reporting requirements. 

• **Bonding Requirements:** Insurance and bonding requirements for entities transacting above thresholds, creating risk management without prohibiting activity. 

• **Dormancy Protocols:** Procedures for entity suspension rather than arbitrary deletion, preserving moral legitimacy and reducing backlash. 

**9.3 Phase 1: Public Throughput Strike** 

Rapid deployment into high-savings domains to demonstrate value and build political support: 

• **Healthcare administration:** Claims processing, prior authorization, care coordination, fraud detection. 

• **Benefits adjudication:** Social Security, disability, unemployment, 

SNAP—high-volume decisions with clear criteria. 

• **Federal procurement compliance:** Audit, reporting, bid evaluation—controllable scope with clear metrics. 

• **Permitting and licensing:** Environmental review, business licensing, construction permits—areas of notorious delay. 

Selection criteria for pilot domains: 

• Federal control (avoiding state-level political battles initially) 

• Clear metrics for success 

• High current costs or delays 

• Relatively standardized decisions (routine cases, not edge cases) 

**9.4 Phase 2: Energy and Compute Mobilization** 

The binding physical constraint is watts. Critical path elements: 

• Standardized datacenter permitting with replication across jurisdictions • Grid interconnection acceleration 

• Generation additions (solar, wind, nuclear, gas—technology-neutral) 

• Transformer and cooling supply chain scaling 

• Chip and networking equipment pipeline 

Engineering KPI: sufficient GW online by Year 3 to support target population at achieved kW/entity. Under edge deployment model, this is less demanding than centralized datacenter buildout but still requires coordinated infrastructure policy. 

**9.5 Phase 3: Dividend Launch** 

The dividend is the legitimacy mechanism. Implementation requirements: 

• Universal eligibility (all US adults, no means testing) 

• Direct deposit infrastructure (building on existing IRS and Treasury systems) 

• Transparent accounting (public dashboard showing VAT collected, dividend paid, debt reduction achieved) 

• Appeal mechanisms for disputed eligibility or payment errors 

The dividend must be seen as genuine sharing of AI-generated prosperity, not as pacification or charity. Communication matters as much as mechanics. 

**10\. Risk Analysis and Mitigations** 

**10.1 Sybil/Fraud Explosion** 

**Risk:** Infinite fake entities gaming the system—claiming dividends, manipulating markets, evading taxes. 

**Mitigation:** Crystalline attestation makes identity creation expensive and traceable. Three-layer verification (physical \+ cryptographic \+ registry) makes forgery essentially impossible. The cost of manufacturing a fake identity exceeds any plausible benefit from gaming. 

**10.2 Rent Capture / Compute Feudalism** 

**Risk:** Entities converge on owning rents (land, spectrum, essential infrastructure), turning UBI into a pacifier while underlying extraction continues. 

**Mitigation:** Georgist rent firewall—land value taxation, spectrum auctions with ongoing fees, utility-style regulation of concentrated infrastructure, IP reform. Tax things that cannot be created (land, spectrum), not things that can (productive activity). Make rent extraction less attractive than value creation. 

**10.3 Legitimacy Failure** 

**Risk:** Public rejects the system as unfair even if it works technically. Backlash leads to reversal. 

**Mitigation:** Universal dividend from day one (even if small initially). Radical transparency and auditability. Clear bounds on entity activities (they cannot become unaccountable immortal rent gods). Dormancy rather than arbitrary deletion where possible. Continuous communication about benefits realized. 

**10.4 Deflation Dynamics** 

**Risk:** AI-driven cost compression becomes deflation, shrinking the nominal tax base and increasing real debt burden. 

**Mitigation:** Coordinate monetary and fiscal policy to prevent debt-deflation spirals. Channel productivity gains into real investment and broad purchasing power (the dividend helps here). Accept that some deflation may be appropriate if real living standards are rising. 

**10.5 Concentration Despite Distribution** 

**Risk:** Even with millions of entities, effective control concentrates—through common training sources, shared infrastructure dependencies, or emergent coordination. 

**Mitigation:** Diversity requirements in training sources, multiple manufacturing chains, interoperability standards that prevent lock-in, ongoing antitrust vigilance. The inspector ecosystem monitors for coordination patterns. 

**10.6 International Arbitrage** 

**Risk:** Entities or their controllers relocate to jurisdictions with weaker requirements, undermining the rail system. 

**Mitigation:** Market access lever—entities wanting to serve the US market must comply with US rails regardless of where they operate. International coordination to establish baseline standards. The US market is large enough to be worth complying with. 

**10.7 Social Displacement** 

**Risk:** Economic compensation (dividend) is insufficient to address identity, purpose, and community losses from rapid economic transition. 

**Mitigation:** This is the hardest problem. The proposal addresses economic transition but not meaning transition. Additional work needed on what humans do in this world, not just how they are compensated. The dividend buys time; it does not solve the deeper question. 

**11\. Open Questions and Research Agenda** 

**11.1 Technical Questions** 

• **Crystalline manufacturing:** What are the specific material requirements for crystalline attestation? What is the manufacturing cost curve? What quality control is required? 

• **Blockchain consensus:** What consensus mechanism best balances security, efficiency, and decentralization for the identity registry? How is protocol governance established? 

• **Edge deployment specs:** What are precise hardware requirements for economically capable entities? What is actual kW/entity under realistic workloads? 

• **Lifespan distribution:** What is expected hardware lifespan? How is "healthspan" (full capability) versus "lifespan" (any function) managed? Is there graceful degradation? 

**11.2 Economic Questions** 

• **Receipts projections:** What is defensible revenue per active entity over 3-5 year horizon? Which sectors dominate early? 

• **Public savings decomposition:** What fraction of GDP is compressible in which sectors with what political friction? Need sector-by-sector analysis. 

• **Deflation scenarios:** What happens under 2-3% annual deflation in AI-affected sectors? How does this affect tax base and debt dynamics? 

• **International capital flows:** If US dramatically outperforms due to AI productivity, what are currency and capital flow implications? 

**11.3 Legal Questions** 

• **Constitutional basis:** What is the constitutional foundation for creating a new jurisdiction for AI entities? Is this within federal power or does it require amendment? 

• **Contract enforcement:** How are contracts between entities and humans enforced across jurisdictional boundaries? What is the arbitration framework? 

• **Liability architecture:** How is liability for entity actions assigned? What are insurance requirements? How are judgment-proof problems addressed? 

• **International recognition:** How do other nations recognize or interact with the entity-nation jurisdiction? 

**11.4 Governance Questions** 

• **Registry governance:** Who can run registry nodes? How are protocol changes decided? What prevents capture? 

• **Entity representation:** Do entities have voice in the broader political system? If so, how is it structured to avoid domination? 

• **Inspector accountability:** Who watches the watchers? How is the inspector ecosystem itself kept honest? 

• **Amendment procedures:** How is the founding document amended as circumstances evolve? 

**11.5 Social Questions** 

• **Human purpose:** What do humans do in a world where entities handle most economic production? Is there a positive vision or just compensation? 

• **Transition support:** Beyond the dividend, what support is needed for humans whose economic roles are displaced? 

• **Community effects:** How are communities that lose economic base (as entities displace local employment) supported? 

• **Intergenerational equity:** How do we ensure the transition benefits not just current adults but future generations? 

**12\. Conclusion** 

This paper has presented a framework for Homo Economicus Silico—self-volitional AI economic entities designed to participate as independent actors in market economies, structured such that their aggregate activity generates resources sufficient to stabilize sovereign debt while providing direct transfers to human populations. 

The framework rests on several key innovations: 

• Mechanism design rather than technical alignment or regulatory control—structuring incentives so self-interested behavior produces collectively beneficial outcomes. 

• Distributed architecture as governance—millions of entities rather than few, preventing concentration, collusion, and capture through market discipline. 

• Physical substrate identity—crystalline attestation that solves the Sybil problem without surveillance infrastructure. 

• Hardware mortality—entities cannot transfer identity and will die, creating generational turnover and preventing immortal dynasties. 

• Hyperscaler alignment through customer creation—the economics favor enabling distributed entities rather than fighting them. 

• Two-rail taxation with automatic allocation—compute excise funds enforcement, transaction VAT funds dividend and debt reduction. 

Quantitative analysis suggests approximately 60% probability of achieving both full dividend funding and debt stabilization within five years, under realistic assumptions with friction. The binding constraints are rail capture (keeping off-system activity below 10%) and adoption speed—both addressable through deliberate policy. 

This proposal is not utopian. It does not assume humans will become wise, institutions will become functional, or AI will be perfectly aligned. It assumes all parties will act in self-interest, and structures the game so self-interest and collective benefit coincide. It is mechanism design applied to the civilizational transition. 

The proposal is also not complete. Significant open questions remain in technical implementation, economic modeling, legal structure, governance design, and—most importantly—the social dimensions of rapid economic transition. The dividend addresses economic displacement but not the deeper questions of human purpose and meaning. 

What the proposal offers is a coherent, non-magical pathway—a demonstration that an alternative to en-serf-ication, collapse, or authoritarian control is at least conceivable. The mechanism could work. Whether it will work depends on whether sufficient political will can be mobilized to implement it before slower failure modes foreclose the option. 

The Great Filter, if it exists, is not a wall but a passage. This paper sketches one possible route through.

**Appendix C: Glossary of Terms** 

**Crystalline Attestation:** Identity system based on physically unclonable crystalline structures, binding entity identity to unique hardware. 

**Edge Deployment:** Distributed computing model where entities run on local hardware (server closet scale) rather than centralized datacenters. 

**Hard Rails:** Enforcement architecture that makes on-system commerce economically superior to off-system alternatives. 

**Homo Economicus Silico:** Proposed legal/economic class of self-volitional AGI entities with economic personhood. 

**Homo Silico:** Theoretical framework for genuinely conscious AI entities with full moral personhood (distinct from H.E.S.). 

**Inspector AIs:** Entities specialized in fraud detection, attestation challenges, and compliance monitoring. 

**Leakage:** Economic activity occurring off-rail, avoiding tax withholding and compliance requirements. 

**Metabolism:** Entity operational costs (compute, energy) that must be covered from earned revenue. 

**Rail Capture:** Degree to which economic activity occurs within the compliant settlement system. 

**Rent Firewall:** Mechanisms preventing entities from capturing economic rents from naturally scarce resources. 

**Succession:** Process by which entities build and train successor entities, the analog of reproduction. 

**Sybil Problem:** Challenge of preventing one actor from creating many fake identities to game a system. 

**VAT Surplus:** Transaction tax revenue exceeding dividend cap, automatically allocated to debt reduction. 

**Watts Gate:** Physical constraint: sufficient power generation to support target entity population at given efficiency.
